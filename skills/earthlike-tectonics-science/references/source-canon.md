# Source Canon

Use this as the default citation stack for Earth-like tectonic simulation work. Prioritize textbooks for first-principles framing, then papers/datasets for parameterization and diagnostics details.

## 1. Core Textbooks

1. Turcotte, D. L., and Schubert, G. (2014, 3rd ed.). *Geodynamics*. Cambridge University Press.
- Use for mantle convection, lithosphere mechanics, flexure, and thermal evolution.
2. Kearey, P., Klepeis, K. A., and Vine, F. J. (2009, 3rd ed.). *Global Tectonics*. Wiley-Blackwell.
- Use for plate boundary processes, mountain belts, and basin evolution.
3. Fowler, C. M. R. (2005, 2nd ed.). *The Solid Earth: An Introduction to Global Geophysics*. Cambridge University Press.
- Use for geophysical constraints and Earth structure context.
4. Schubert, G., Turcotte, D. L., and Olson, P. (2001). *Mantle Convection in the Earth and Planets*. Cambridge University Press.
- Use for convection-style assumptions and plate-mantle coupling background.

## 2. Foundational Papers and Reviews

1. Wilson, J. T. (1966). Did the Atlantic close and then re-open? *Nature*, 211, 676-681. [doi:10.1038/211676a0](https://doi.org/10.1038/211676a0)
- Use for Wilson-cycle framing of ocean opening/closing.
2. Forsyth, D., and Uyeda, S. (1975). On the relative importance of driving forces of plate motion. *Tectonophysics*, 13-24. [doi:10.1016/0040-1951(75)90070-6](https://doi.org/10.1016/0040-1951(75)90070-6)
- Use for slab-pull/ridge-push force-balance intuition.
3. McKenzie, D. (1978). Some remarks on the development of sedimentary basins. *Earth and Planetary Science Letters*, 40, 25-32. [doi:10.1016/0012-821X(78)90071-5](https://doi.org/10.1016/0012-821X(78)90071-5)
- Use for rifting/subsidence first-order basin modeling.
4. Stern, R. J. (2002). Subduction zones. *Reviews of Geophysics*, 40(4). [doi:10.1029/2001RG000108](https://doi.org/10.1029/2001RG000108)
- Use for convergent-margin process taxonomy and arc/subduction behavior.
5. Cawood, P. A., Kröner, A., and Collins, W. J. (2009). Accretionary orogens through Earth history. *Geological Society Special Publications*, 318, 1-36. [doi:10.1144/SP318.1](https://doi.org/10.1144/SP318.1)
- Use for terrane accretion and long-term continental growth framing.
6. Nance, R. D., Murphy, J. B., and Santosh, M. (2014). The supercontinent cycle: A retrospective essay. *Gondwana Research*, 25(1), 4-29. [doi:10.1016/j.gr.2012.12.026](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.gr.2012.12.026)
- Use for supercontinent assembly/dispersal uncertainty and competing mechanisms.

## 3. Global Data Products and Agency References

1. Müller, R. D., et al. (2008). Age, spreading rates, and spreading asymmetry of the world’s ocean crust. *Geochemistry, Geophysics, Geosystems*, 9(4). [doi:10.1029/2007GC001743](https://doi.org/10.1029/2007GC001743)
- Use for seafloor-age and spreading-rate context in oceanic lifecycle checks.
2. USGS FAQ: How fast do tectonic plates move? [https://www.usgs.gov/faqs/how-fast-do-tectonic-plates-move](https://www.usgs.gov/faqs/how-fast-do-tectonic-plates-move)
- Use for public-facing rate intuition and communication baseline.
3. NOAA Ocean Exploration: What age is the seafloor? [https://oceanexplorer.noaa.gov/facts/seafloor-age.html](https://oceanexplorer.noaa.gov/facts/seafloor-age.html)
- Use for high-level oceanic-crust age context and ridge generation framing.

## 4. Optional Worldbuilding Heuristic

## 5. Citation Policy for Simulator Work

- Cite at least one textbook/review source for process-level claims.
- Cite at least one dataset/agency source for numeric bounds when available.
- Mark unvalidated assumptions as `heuristic`.
